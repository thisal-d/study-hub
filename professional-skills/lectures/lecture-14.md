## Why This Topic Matters

Computing technology magnifies both positive innovation and potential harm, making intellectual property, innovation, and risk management essential for responsible digital development. Ethical and inclusive cultures support responsible innovation and prevent unethical practices like code theft or biased systems.

- IP ensures creators are respected and rewarded.

- Innovation must be ethical, inclusive, and socially responsible.

- Risk management protects organisations, users, and society from digital harm.

## Introduction

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is transforming every industry, from healthcare to finance, transportation, education, and public administration. With this transformation comes new responsibilities for IT professionals.

## Key Themes

- AI ethics ensures AI systems are fair, safe, transparent, and accountable.

- Responsible innovation balances creativity with protecting society.

- Career readiness means being prepared for an AI-powered job market, with skills in ethics, regulation, teamwork, and adaptability.

## Ethics as a Framework for AI Practice

## What Ethics Means in AI

- Ethics guides decisions when technology can impact millions. AI systems make predictions, recommendations, and automated decisions, so they must be designed responsibly.

## Core Ethical Questions

- Is the AI system fair to all groups?

- Does it protect privacy and user rights?

- Does it reinforce discrimination or inequality?

- Is the system transparent and explainable?

## Ethics as a Framework for AI Practice

## Insights from Baase (A Gift of Fire)

- Technology amplifies the consequences of human decisions.

- AI creates new risks: loss of privacy, surveillance, misinformation.

- Ethical decision-making must happen before deployment.

## Insights from Belonging

- Inclusion leads to ethical outcomes.

- AI systems built without diverse input risk reinforcing bias.

- Responsible AI requires listening to underrepresented communities.

## Emerging Trends in AI Ethics

## 1. Bias & Fairness in AI

- AI models often inherit bias from historical data.

- Example: Loan approval systems rejecting minorities.

- Sri Lankan example: Biased credit-scoring models.

## 2. Explainable AI (XAI)

- Users require transparent decisions.

- In banking and healthcare, explainability is critical.

## 3. AI Safety & Governance

- EU AI Act, UNESCO guidelines, global safety debates.

- Sri Lanka beginning to adopt ethical AI frameworks.

## Emerging Trends in AI Ethics

## 4. Deepfakes & Misinformation

- AI-generated misinformation threatens democracy.

- Sri Lanka facing election-related misinformation risks.

## 5. Data Privacy in AI

- AI models require large datasets -> privacy challenges.

- PDPA regulates how Sri Lankan companies handle data.

## Responsible Innovation

## Definition

- Responsible innovation ensures technology benefits society while minimizing harm.

## Principles

- Fairness

- Transparency

- Accountability

- Inclusiveness

- Sustainability

## Responsible Innovation

- Ethical foresight: anticipate unintended consequences.

- Accountability: developers must prevent harm.

- Inclusive teams create better, fairer technologies.

- Innovation should empower, not exclude, communities.

## Global Examples

- Google's AI ethics failures (e.g., firing ethical AI researchers).

- Microsoft's Responsible AI initiatives.

## Sri Lankan Examples

- AI chatbots in government services.

- Telecom algorithms analysing customer behaviour.

- Need for transparency in automated decision-making.

## Professional Responsibilities

## Towards Clients

- Provide safe, transparent AI systems.

- Protect client data.

## Towards Employers

- Follow ethical guidelines.

- Report risks or unethical use of AI.

## Towards Colleagues

- Promote inclusive and respectful teamwork.

- Avoid bias or discrimination in innovation.

## Towards Society

- Create AI that benefits all communities.

- Prevent harmful or manipulative AI (e.g., political targeting).
