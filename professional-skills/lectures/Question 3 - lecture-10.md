## What Are Ethics?

- Ethics are guidelines that help us decide what is right and wrong. In IT, this applies to designing software, handling data, creating algorithms, and managing systems. Because technology can affect millions of people at once, small decisions can have huge impacts.

## Key Ideas

- Technology creates new moral questions (e.g., surveillance, AI profiling).

- People can lose privacy without noticing.

- Ethical decisions must consider long-term impact.

- Ethics means treating all users and colleagues with fairness and respect.

- Inclusion and belonging reduce discrimination, which is important in the design of systems.

- A strong ethical culture encourages responsible decisions.

## Ethics as a Framework for Responsible IT Practice

## Why This Matters to IT Students

- You will build systems that store personal data, control finances, automate decisions, or even influence elections. Without ethics:

- Users can be harmed.

- Organizations lose trust.

- You become legally responsible.

## Professional Responsibilities (Clients, Employers, Colleagues, Society)

## 1. Responsibility to Clients

- Deliver accurate, reliable systems.

- Communicate honestly about limitations.

- Protect client data at all costs.

## 2. Responsibility to Employers

- Follow internal security and data-handling policies.

- Report security threats or unethical behavior.

- Do not misuse company resources.

## Professional Responsibilities (Clients, Employers, Colleagues, Society)

## 3. Responsibility to Colleagues

- Respect their ideas and perspectives.

- Avoid discrimination and support an inclusive culture.

- Help maintain a healthy workplace.

## 4. Responsibility to Society

- Avoid creating harmful systems (e.g., biased AI, spyware).

- Ensure your work respects human rights.

- Support sustainability and long-term welfare.

These responsibilities come directly from ACM,IEEE, and CSSL codes.

## Ethical Decision-Making Models

## Stakeholder Analysis

"Consider everyone affected by the decision." - Includes users, non-users, employees, vulnerable groups. - Belonging emphasizes including underrepresented voices.

## Example 1 - AI Hiring System

## Scenario:

A company adopts AI to filter job applications.

Stakeholders: Job applicants, HR team, Company leadership, Underrepresented groups (women, minorities), Society (workplace equality)

## Analysis:

- The algorithm might unintentionally favor certain groups, excluding others. This harms diversity and violates fairness.

## Stakeholder Decision:

- Test for bias

- Use diverse datasets

- Maintain human oversight

- Ensure transparency

## Ethical Decision: Apply Ethical Theories

## 1. Utilitarianism (Outcome-Based) - Maximise benefit, Risk: minority harm

"Choose the option that benefits the most people." - Used for large-scale decisions (e.g., pandemic tracking apps). - Problem: Sometimes harms a small group unfairly.

## Example 1 - Pandemic Tracking App

Scenario: A government launches a mobile app to track COVID-19 exposure. The app collects location + health data from millions of people.

## Benefits (Majority):

- Saves lives by notifying exposed individuals

- Helps healthcare systems prepare

- Reduces spread of infection

## Harms (Minority):

- Some users lose privacy

- Sensitive health data could be misused if leaked

## Utilitarian Conclusion:

Deploy the app because the benefit to society outweighs privacy risks, but with safeguards (encryption, consent, transparency).

## Ethical Decision: Apply Ethical Theories

## 1. Utilitarianism (Outcome-Based) Cont'd

## Example 2 - Automated Traffic Optimization

A city uses AI cameras to reduce traffic congestion. More people benefit through shorter travel times, but some drivers receive wrongly issued fines due to AI errors.

## Utilitarian View:

Implement the system because overall efficiency improves. But also create an appeals process to reduce harm to the minority.

## Ethical Decision: Apply Ethical Theories

## 2. Deontology (Duty-Based): Rules matter. Rights must not be violated

"Some actions are always right or wrong."- Violating privacy is wrong even if it benefits society. Useful for identifying non-negotiable rules.

## Example 1 - Employee Data Privacy

Scenario: A company wants to secretly monitor employee messages to improve productivity.

## Utilitarian View:

"Better productivity benefits the company and customers."

## Deontological View:

Secret monitoring violates employee rights, breaks trust, and ignores consent. Therefore, it is always wrong, even if it boosts productivity.

## Ethical Decision: Apply Ethical Theories

## 2. Deontology (Duty-Based) Cont'd

## Example 2 - Manipulative Dark Patterns

- A website uses confusing buttons to trick users into subscribing.

- Outcome: Company profits increase (positive result).

But: Trickery violates honesty, transparency, autonomy.

## Deontological Conclusion:

Unethical regardless of financial benefits.

## IT Laws & Data Protection

## Global Laws

## General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)

## 1 It applies across all EU countries

One single regulation protects all EU citizens, covering a population of more than 447 million people.

## 2. It applies to companies outside the EU

Even Sri Lankan companies must follow GDPR if they collect, store, or process data of people in the EU (example: tourism, e-commerce, online services).

## 3. It has strict rules and heavy enforcement

Companies must prove they handle data responsibly, securely, and with transparency.

## IT Laws & Data Protection

## User Rights Under GDPR

- GDPR gives individuals powerful control over their personal information.

## Right to Access

- Users can ask:

"What information do you have about me, and why?"

Companies must provide a clear, free copy.

## Right to Correction (Rectification)

- If information is incorrect or outdated, users can demand changes.

Example:

Wrong address or wrong NIC/passport number $ \rightarrow $ must be fixed immediately.

## IT Laws & Data Protection

## Right to Deletion (Right to Be Forgotten)

- Users can request their data to be erased when:

- They no longer use the service

- The company has no valid reason to keep the data

- Consent is withdrawn

Example:

Deleting a social media profile permanently.

## Right to Data Portability

- Users can request their data in a machine-readable format to move it to another service.

- Example:

Downloading your Facebook data to transfer to another platform.

## IT Laws & Data Protection

## Heavy Fines for Violations (Why Companies Fear GDPR)

GDPR can impose enormous penalties, including:

- Up to €20 million OR 4% of a company's global annual revenue (Whichever is higher)

- This means:

- Google was fined 50 million

- Amazon was fined 746 million

- Smaller companies also face punishment for leaks or misuse

- These heavy consequences make organizations take data protection seriously.

## IT Laws & Data Protection

## Global Laws

## California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA)

- Customers can opt out of data selling.

- Companies must disclose what data they collect.

## IT Laws & Data Protection

## Sri Lankan Laws

## Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA) No.9 of 2022

Key points:

- Consent is required before collecting data.

- Users can request correction or deletion.

- Organizations must secure personal data.

- A Data Protection Authority will enforce rules.

## Other Important Sri Lankan Acts

- Computer Crimes Act (2007): Criminal offenses related to hacking.

- Electronic Transactions Act (2006): Legitimizes digital signatures.

- Right to Information Act (2016): Citizens can request public data.

## Your Legal Responsibilities as an IT Professional

- Use data only for the stated purpose.

- Delete data when no longer needed.

- Keep users informed.
