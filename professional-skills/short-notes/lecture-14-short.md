## 🧭 Ethics as a Framework for AI Practice

> **Ethics guides decisions when technology can impact millions. AI systems make predictions, recommendations, and automated decisions — so they must be designed responsibly.**

### Core Ethical Questions Every AI Developer Must Ask:
- Is the AI system **fair (සාධාරණ)** to all groups?
- Does it protect **privacy (රහස්‍යතාව)** and user rights?
- Does it reinforce **discrimination (වෙනස්කොට සැලකීම)** or inequality?
- Is the system **transparent (විනිවිද — clear and explainable so users can understand how and why a decision was made)** and **explainable (ෙ-පැහැදිලි)**?

### Key Insights:

| **Source** | **Key Lesson** |
|---|---|
| **Baase — "A Gift of Fire"** | Technology amplifies human decisions; AI creates new risks of privacy loss, surveillance, and misinformation; ethical decisions must happen **before** deployment |
| **Belonging** | Inclusion leads to ethical outcomes; AI systems built without diverse input risk reinforcing bias; responsible AI requires listening to underrepresented communities |

## 🌐 Emerging Trends in AI Ethics

### 1. Bias & Fairness in AI (AI-ගත-ෙ — AI systems that produce unfair outcomes for certain groups, often because the training data reflects historical inequality)

- AI models often inherit **bias (පක්ෂපාතිත්වය — a systematic tendency to favour one group or outcome over another in an unfair way)** from historical data
- *Example (Global):* Loan approval systems rejecting minority applicants
- *Example (Sri Lanka):* Biased **credit-scoring models (ණය-ශ්‍රේෂ්ඨ මාදිලි — AI systems used by banks to decide if someone can get a loan; if biased, they may unfairly reject certain groups)** in banks

### 2. Explainable AI / XAI (පැහැදිලි-AI — AI systems designed to explain their decisions in a way that humans can understand, increasing trust and accountability)

- Users require **transparent decisions** — especially in banking, healthcare, and justice
- In high-stakes areas, users must understand **why** the AI made a specific recommendation

### 3. AI Safety & Governance (AI ආරක්ෂාව හා රාජ්‍ය-පාලනය — rules, laws, and institutional practices that ensure AI systems are safe, accountable, and do not cause harm)

- **EU AI Act** — global standard classifying AI by risk level and setting strict requirements
- **UNESCO guidelines** — international principles for ethical AI
- Sri Lanka beginning to adopt ethical AI frameworks

### 4. Deepfakes & Misinformation (ගැඹුරු-ෙ — AI-generated fake videos or audio that are realistic enough to make people appear to say or do things they never said or did)

- **Deepfakes (ගැඹුරු-ෙ)** = AI-generated fake videos/audio that look realistic
- AI-generated **misinformation (ෙ-තතු — false or inaccurate information that is spread, often unintentionally, causing confusion or harm)** threatens democracy
- *Sri Lanka context:* Election-related misinformation risks are growing

### 5. Data Privacy in AI (AI-ගත-රහස්‍යතාව — the challenge of protecting individuals' personal information when it is collected and used to train AI systems)

- AI models require **large datasets (දත්ත-සෑදොල් — very large collections of data used to train AI systems)** → creates serious privacy challenges
- **PDPA** regulates how Sri Lankan companies handle personal data
- Risk: individuals' private data collected without meaningful consent

## 🚀 Responsible Innovation (වගකිවෙ-නවෝත්පාදනය)

> **"Responsible innovation ensures technology benefits society while minimizing harm."**

### Five Principles:

| **Principle** | **What It Means** |
|---|---|
| **Fairness (සාධාරණභාවය — ensuring AI systems treat all people equally and do not produce outcomes that unfairly advantage or disadvantage certain groups)** | AI must not discriminate or systematically favour one group |
| **Transparency (විනිවිදභාවය — making sure users understand how an AI system works and why it makes specific decisions)** | Users can understand how and why decisions are made |
| **Accountability (වගවීම — developers and organizations must accept responsibility for the impact their AI systems have on people)** | Developers must accept responsibility for harms caused |
| **Inclusiveness (ඇතුළත් කිරීම — involving diverse people in the design process so that AI systems work well for everyone, not just the majority)** | Diverse teams create fairer, more complete technologies |
| **Sustainability (තිරසාරභාවය)** | Innovation must not damage the environment or future generations |

### Additional Principles:
- **Ethical foresight (ආචාර ධර්ම-ෙ — thinking ahead about what might go wrong with a technology and taking steps to prevent harm before deployment)** — anticipate unintended consequences before deployment
- **Inclusive teams** create better, fairer technologies
- Innovation should **empower (බලය-දීම — give individuals and communities greater control, opportunity, and capability through technology)**, not exclude, communities

### Global Examples:
- **Google** — faced criticism for firing ethical AI researchers and for racial bias in image recognition
- **Microsoft** — Responsible AI initiatives; accessibility-focused AI products

### Sri Lankan Examples:
- **AI chatbots** in government services
- **Telecom algorithms** analysing customer behaviour
- Need for **transparency (විනිවිද)** in automated decision-making systems

## 🧑‍💼 Professional Responsibilities in the AI Age

| **Responsibility To** | **What You Must Do** |
|---|---|
| **Clients** | Provide safe, transparent AI systems; protect client data |
| **Employers** | Follow ethical guidelines; report risks or unethical use of AI |
| **Colleagues** | Promote inclusive and respectful teamwork; avoid bias or discrimination in innovation |
| **Society** | Create AI that benefits all communities; prevent harmful or **manipulative AI (හසුරු-AI — AI designed to influence people's beliefs or behaviour in ways that are deceptive or against their own interests, such as political targeting)** |

## 🏛️ Legal & Regulatory Frameworks

### Global Laws & Guidelines:

| **Law / Guideline** | **Key Focus** |
|---|---|
| **EU AI Act** | Classifies AI by risk; bans certain high-risk uses |
| **GDPR (EU)** | Data protection and privacy |
| **UNESCO AI Ethics** | Global principles for fair and responsible AI |
| **U.S. AI Bill of Rights** | Protects citizens from harmful AI practices |

### Sri Lankan Context:

| **Law** | **Relevance to AI** |
|---|---|
| **PDPA Act No.9 of 2022** | Controls how AI systems can collect and use personal data |
| **Computer Crimes Act** | Covers hacking and unauthorized system access |
| **Electronic Transactions Act** | Legal framework for digital and AI-powered transactions |
| **No dedicated AI law yet** | There is a growing need for AI-specific governance in Sri Lanka |

## 📚 Case Studies

| **Case Study** | **Ethical Issue** | **Lesson** |
|---|---|---|
| **COMPAS Algorithm (USA)** | AI used to predict criminal behaviour; found **biased (ෙ)** against minorities | Fairness and accountability in automated decisions |
| **Google Photos Tagging Scandal** | AI labelled Black people as "gorillas" — **bias in training data (ෙ-දත්ත — training examples that over-represent some groups and under-represent others, causing the AI to make unfair predictions)** | Diverse, representative datasets are essential |
| **Sri Lankan Facial Recognition Pilot** | Used in public spaces; concerns about consent, bias, privacy | Need for consent and transparent governance |
| **AI in Sri Lankan Banking** | Automated **credit-scoring (ණය-ශ්‍රේෂ්ඨ)** risks unfair rejection and lack of transparency | Transparent, auditable AI models required |
| **Whistleblowing in AI Teams** | Employees report unethical training data practices | Need for **psychological safety (මනෝවිද්‍යාත්මක ආරක්ෂාව — the feeling that it is safe to speak up or report concerns without fear of punishment)** in workplace culture |
