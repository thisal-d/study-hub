## 🎯 What is Power? (බලය යනු කුමක්ද?)

> **Max Weber (pioneering sociologist):**
> *"Power is the probability that one actor within a social relationship will be in a position to carry out his own will despite resistance."*

- **Power (බලය — the ability to influence what another person does or thinks, even if they resist)** = the ability to influence another person
- **Authority (බලධිකාරිය — the formal, official right to make decisions or give orders, based on a person's position or role)** = the right to influence another person

> ⚠️ **Key Difference:** A manager has *authority* by job title. But a popular team member may have *power* through personality, even without a title.

> 💡 **Real-World Example:** A senior textile engineer at MAS Holdings has **power** through knowledge — workers follow their advice not because they must, but because the engineer knows best. This is **Expert Power**.

---

## 🏛️ The Milgram Experiments

- Conducted by **Stanley Milgram** at Yale University, early 1960s
- Tested **obedience to authority (බලධිකාරිට අවනත වීම — the tendency of people to follow instructions from someone in a position of power, even when uncomfortable)**
- Result: People follow authority figures even when told to do something that makes them uncomfortable
- Key lesson: **Formal authority** has enormous influence over behaviour

## 🔑 The Five Forms of Power (French & Raven, 1959)

| **Type of Power** | **Definition** | **Real-World Example** |
|---|---|---|
| **Reward Power (ත්‍යාග-බලය — power based on the ability to give people what they want, such as bonuses, promotions, or praise)** | Ability to control rewards the target wants | Manager giving bonuses, promotions, overseas training |
| **Legitimate Power (නීත්‍යානුකූල-බලය — power that comes from an official position or job title that both sides accept as valid)** | Power based on official position; mutually agreed | Factory Manager assigns shifts; employees follow because of his position |
| **Coercive Power (බලහත්කාර-බලය — power based on the ability to punish or cause an unpleasant experience for someone who does not comply)** | Ability to cause an unpleasant experience | Supervisor warns worker of disciplinary action for missing quotas |
| **Expert Power (විශේෂඥ-බලය — power that comes from having specialist knowledge or skills that others need and rely on)** | Power based on knowledge the target needs | Textile engineer at MAS Holdings advises on fabric optimization |
| **Referent / Charismatic Power (චරිශ්ම-බලය — power based on personal charm, likability, and interpersonal attraction; timing matters greatly)** | Power based on personal charm and admiration | A team leader rallies her team through passion and trust, not title |

> 💡 **Sri Lankan Context:**
> - **Karu Jayasuriya** (Mayor of Colombo, 1997–1999) → **Expert Power** via innovative waste management → 60,310 preferential votes (2010)
> - **Upeksha Swarnamali** (actress) → **Charismatic Power** → 81,350 preferential votes (2010); chose not to contest in 2015 — charisma faded
> - **Ranjan Ramanayake** (actor) → **Charismatic Power** → 216,463 preferential votes (2015)

> ⚠️ **Important:** Charismatic Power depends on **timing (කාලය — the right moment; influence that worked at one time may not work later)**

---

## ⚖️ Consequences of Power

- **Positive:** motivation, efficiency, team growth
- **Negative:** abuse, manipulation, loss of trust

> 💡 A leader using only **Coercive Power** (threats and punishments) will create fear, causing employees to underperform or leave.

## 🏢 Political Behaviour in Organizations (සංවිධාන තුළ දේශපාලනික හැසිරීම)

> **"Political Behaviour = Actions not officially sanctioned (authorized) by an organization, taken to influence others in order to meet one's personal goals."** — *Stephen P. Robbins*

- **Political behaviour (දේශපාලනික හැසිරීම — unofficial actions taken within an organization to build influence and achieve personal or group goals)** is simply a fact of life in all organizations
- It is NOT always negative — used wisely, it builds influence and networks

> 💡 **Key Book:** *"How to Win Friends and Influence People"* — Dale Carnegie (1998)
> *"Always make the other person feel important…and do it sincerely."*

## 🎯 Political Strategies for Attaining Power

| **Strategy** | **Simple Explanation** |
|---|---|
| **Taking counsel (උපදේශ ලබා ගැනීම — actively seeking advice from experienced, trusted people before making important decisions)** | Seek advice from experienced people |
| **Maintaining maneuverability (ක්‍රියාකාරී නම්‍යශීලිත්වය — keeping your options open so you can adapt if circumstances change)** | Keep options open; don't lock yourself in one position |
| Promoting limited communication | Share only what is necessary |
| **Exhibiting confidence (විශ්වාසය ප්‍රකාශ කිරීම — appearing self-assured in decisions and actions)** | Always appear sure of yourself |
| Controlling access to information | Be the key person others rely on for important data |
| **Creating sponsor-protégé relationships (ඉදිරිපත් කිරීම — having a mentor who supports and sponsors your career growth)** | Be mentored by or mentor others |
| **Neutralizing potential opposition (විරෝධය අඩු කිරීම — reducing or removing threats from people who might block your goals)** | Reduce threats before they grow |
| **Forming a winning coalition (ජයග්‍රාහී සන්ධානය — joining forces with different groups or parties who share a common goal, even if they disagree on other things)** | Build alliances to achieve a shared goal |
| Developing expertise | Become the expert others rely on |
| Building personal stature | Improve your reputation and visibility |
| **Committing the uncommitted (නිරාසක්ත අය සම්බන්ධ කිරීම — engaging people who are not yet emotionally invested and giving them a sense of ownership)** | Give disengaged people a reason to care |
| Withdrawing from petty disputes | Don't waste energy on minor conflicts |

---

### 🤝 Strategy: "Committing the Uncommitted" — MAS Holdings Example
- MAS launched a **"Voice of the Employee"** program
- Workers could suggest improvements; recognized suggestions were **implemented publicly**
- Previously disengaged workers gained **a sense of ownership** → increased commitment and performance

---

### 🏆 Strategy: "Forming a Winning Coalition" — 2015 Presidential Election Example
- **Maithripala Sirisena** broke away from the Rajapaksa regime
- United **multiple opposition parties** (UNP, JHU, TNA) + civil society groups
- Appealed to minorities and moderate Sinhalese voters around a **common goal: democratic change**
- ✅ A classic example of a winning coalition achieving a strategic objective

## 🏭 Organizational Culture (සංවිධාන සංස්කෘතිය)

> **Definition:** *"A pattern of basic assumptions, invented, discovered, or developed by a given group as it learns to cope with its problems of external adaptation and internal integration."*

- **Organizational Culture (සංවිධාන සංස්කෘතිය — the shared values, beliefs, norms, and ways of working that define how people in an organization think, behave, and interact)** = the personality of an organization
- It teaches new members the **correct way to perceive, think, and feel** about problems

### 6 Important Characteristics

| **#** | **Characteristic** | **Example** |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Observed behavioural regularities (ශ්‍රේෂ්ඨ-හැසිරීම් ක්‍රම — the common ways people behave, speak, and interact within the organization)** | Common language and terminology used inside the company |
| 2 | **Norms (ප්‍රමිතීන් — unwritten rules about how much work to do, how to dress, and how to behave with colleagues)** | Guidelines on expected work output; dress code |
| 3 | **Dominant values (ප්‍රධාන අගයන් — the most important principles the organization actively promotes and practices)** | High product quality, low absenteeism, high efficiency |
| 4 | **Philosophy (දර්ශනය — the organization's deeply held beliefs about how employees and customers should be treated)** | "Customer is always right" or "employees come first" |
| 5 | **Rules (නීති රීති)** | Formal policies and procedures everyone must follow |
| 6 | **Organizational climate (සංවිධාන වාතාවරණය — the overall feel or atmosphere of the workplace; how members interact with each other and with outsiders)** | Open and friendly? Rigid and formal? |

> 💡 **Real-World Example:** Google's culture encourages creativity (free food, flexible hours, open spaces) → attracts top talent → drives better product development.
